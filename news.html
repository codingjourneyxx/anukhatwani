The Resurgence of Handcrafted Furniture in Contemporary Spaces

By Anu Khatwani — Design Editor

In an era dominated by mass production and algorithmic design, a quiet revolution is taking place in the studios and workshops of India's most discerning furniture makers. The return to handcrafted furniture represents more than mere nostalgia — it signals a profound shift in how we understand the relationship between maker, material, and the spaces we inhabit.

Artisans working with indigenous woods such as sheesham, teak, and mango have begun reimagining traditional joinery techniques through a contemporary lens. The dovetail joint, once hidden beneath layers of veneer, now becomes an expressive element — celebrated rather than concealed. This approach reflects a broader cultural movement toward transparency in craft and design.

The economics of handcraft are shifting too. Collectors and homeowners increasingly view bespoke furniture not as an indulgence but as an investment in sustainability, provenance, and emotional durability — qualities that no factory floor can replicate.

Why Biophilic Design Is No Longer Optional

Feature — Interiors Desk

The integration of natural elements into built environments has evolved from a trend into an imperative. Research published across multiple disciplines now confirms what designers have intuited for decades: human beings are physiologically and psychologically wired to thrive in spaces that reference the natural world.

Biophilic design extends far beyond potted plants and green walls. It encompasses the quality of light that enters a room, the materiality of surfaces we touch, the acoustic textures that surround us, and the spatial patterns that echo those found in nature. Fractal geometry in ceiling details, circadian lighting systems, and water features integrated into living spaces are becoming standard specifications in luxury residential projects.

Leading architects report that clients now request biophilic principles as a baseline, not a premium addition. The conversation has shifted from "why should we include nature?" to "how deeply can we integrate it?"

Material Honesty: The New Luxury

Opinion — Architecture Column

For decades, luxury in Indian interior design was synonymous with ornamentation — the more gilded, the more marbled, the more intricately carved, the more "luxurious" a space was deemed. This paradigm is fracturing under the weight of a new sensibility that prizes authenticity over ostentation.

Material honesty — the practice of allowing materials to appear as they truly are — has become the defining ethos of sophisticated design in 2026. Exposed concrete reveals the rhythm of its formwork. Brass develops its natural patina rather than being polished to an artificial shine. Stone is left with its natural fissures visible, each crack narrating millennia of geological history.

This shift demands greater skill from designers, not less. When there is nothing to hide behind, every proportion must be exact, every junction precise, every material selection deliberate. The discipline required to achieve simplicity far exceeds that of complexity.

Clients who once requested marble-clad powder rooms now ask for hand-finished lime plaster. The same clients who insisted on crystal chandeliers now specify sculptural pendants in raw clay or oxidised steel. The vocabulary of desire has fundamentally changed — and design must follow.

India's Design Renaissance: How a New Generation of Architects Is Redefining the Built Landscape

By Correspondent — National Feature • 14 min read

Across the subcontinent, a generation of architects and designers educated at the intersection of tradition and technology is producing work that refuses easy categorisation. These practitioners — many under forty, several trained abroad but working resolutely in India — are creating buildings and interiors that speak a distinctly contemporary Indian language without resorting to pastiche.

Their work is characterised by a rigorous attention to climate responsiveness, an embrace of local materials and construction methods, and a willingness to let a building's programme drive its formal expression rather than imposing predetermined aesthetic frameworks. The results are often startling in their originality: a residence in Alibaug that appears to grow from its laterite landscape; a boutique hotel in Rajasthan that reinterprets the haveli without quoting it; a workspace in Bengaluru where the boundary between interior and garden dissolves entirely.

What unites this diverse body of work is a shared conviction that Indian design need not look westward for validation. The vocabulary is emerging from the ground up — informed by Vastu without being bound by it, conversant with modernism without being subservient to it, deeply local yet globally legible. International publications and award juries have taken notice, but the architects themselves remain focused on the work rather than the recognition.

The implications extend beyond architecture. As these designers gain influence, they are reshaping expectations about what Indian spaces can look and feel like — from commercial interiors to public institutions to private homes. The built environment of India in 2030 will look markedly different from that of 2020, and the blueprints for that transformation are being drawn today.

On Patience: The Forgotten Virtue in Modern Design Practice

By Anu Khatwani

There is a velocity to contemporary design practice that troubles me. Projects move from concept to completion at speeds that would have been unimaginable a generation ago. Digital tools compress timelines. Client expectations accelerate. The market demands constant novelty. And yet the best spaces I have inhabited — the rooms that have genuinely altered how I understand comfort, beauty, and belonging — were all products of patience.

Patience in material selection: waiting for the right slab of marble rather than accepting the first available option. Patience in spatial composition: living with a floor plan for weeks before committing to construction. Patience in the relationship with artisans: allowing a craftsman to work at the pace that the material demands rather than the pace the schedule requires.

I am not arguing for indulgence or inefficiency. I am arguing for the kind of rigorous, attentive slowness that produces work capable of enduring — spaces that will still feel right in twenty years, not merely trending this season. The industry speaks endlessly of sustainability in materials. Perhaps it is time we speak of sustainability in process.

Letters to the Editor: Readers Respond to Our Coverage of the Mumbai Design Biennale

Reader Correspondence

Sir — Your coverage of the Mumbai Design Biennale was thorough and welcome, but I must respectfully disagree with your critic's assessment of the Indian Pavilion. Far from being "cautiously retrospective," the pavilion's focus on textile architecture represented a bold reclamation of a medium that Western design discourse has systematically marginalised. The work of the weavers from Maheshwar and Chanderi deserved greater attention than a parenthetical mention.

Madam Editor — Thank you for the feature on emerging landscape architects. As someone who has practiced landscape design in India for thirty years, I was heartened to see the profession receive the editorial attention it deserves. Too often, landscape is treated as an afterthought — the green decoration applied once the "real" architecture is complete. Your article correctly identified it as a foundational design discipline.

Dear Design Insight — I write regarding the article on parametric design tools. While the technology is impressive, I worry that we are training a generation of designers who can operate software but cannot sketch a room in plan by hand. The eye trained through drawing develops spatial instincts that no algorithm can replicate. I hope your publication will continue to champion the hand-drawn alongside the digitally generated.

To the Editors — Your newspaper has become essential reading in our studio. The rigour and independence of your criticism is rare in a field where publications are often indistinguishable from advertising. Long may it continue.
